% ABSTRACT
% ======================================================================
\begin{abstract}
Camera-based remote photoplethysmography (rPPG) has emerged as a promising
modality for contactless physiological monitoring; however, existing systems
rely predominantly on facial landmark heuristics and lack rigorous
cross-modal validation against clinical-grade references. Furthermore, the
integration of real-time physiological telemetry into adaptive Human--AI
interaction remains largely unexplored. This paper presents \textsc{AURA}
(\textit{Affective Understanding \& Responsive Agent}), an end-to-end
framework that bridges this gap by fusing webcam-derived rPPG
signals---processed through a zero-phase fourth-order Butterworth bandpass
filter ($0.7$--$3.0$~Hz)---with consumer smartwatch ground truth, achieving
a Mean Absolute Error (MAE) of $2.15$~BPM under controlled baseline
conditions and a Pearson correlation of $r > 0.96$. The framework introduces
a novel biometric-adaptive feedback loop in which time-domain Heart Rate
Variability (HRV) metrics, specifically the Root Mean Square of Successive
Differences (RMSSD), are injected into a locally hosted Large Language
Model's (LLM) system prompt to dynamically modulate communicative style
between calm and stressed cognitive states. Ablation studies across three
experimental protocols---baseline, illumination stress, and cognitive
stress---demonstrate robust performance degradation characteristics and
validate the system's affective-sensing capability through a statistically
significant RMSSD reduction ($p < 0.01$) during cognitive load. The entire
system operates locally on consumer hardware with zero external API
dependencies, preserving user privacy while enabling real-time
biometric-adaptive AI interaction at sub-second latency.
\end{abstract}

% I. INTRODUCTION

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

The reliable estimation of cardiovascular vital signs from non-contact
optical sensors has been a longstanding objective in ubiquitous
health monitoring~\cite{verkruysse2008remote, poh2011advancements}.
Conventional approaches to camera-based emotion recognition and
physiological assessment have historically depended upon facial Action
Unit (AU) classification, landmark geometry, and expression-based
heuristics---methods that are inherently susceptible to cultural bias,
voluntary suppression, and illumination-dependent failure
modes~\cite{li2014remote, chen2018deepphys}.

This paper posits a fundamental shift from \emph{facial landmarks to
physiological truth}. Rather than inferring affective state from
surface-level facial features, we extract the involuntary blood volume
pulse (BVP) directly from the green channel of a standard RGB webcam
video stream and compute time-domain Heart Rate Variability (HRV)
metrics that serve as objective biomarkers of autonomic nervous system
activity~\cite{shaffer2017overview}. This approach offers two critical
advantages over expression-based methods: (i)~physiological signals
cannot be consciously suppressed, providing a ground-truth indicator of
cognitive load, and (ii)~the rPPG modality is inherently resistant to
the cultural and contextual biases that plague facial expression
recognition~\cite{barrett2019emotional}.

The central contribution of this work is the \textsc{AURA} framework
(\textit{Affective Understanding \& Responsive Agent}), which integrates
three previously disjoint capabilities into a unified real-time system:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Contactless rPPG Signal Processing.} A multi-threaded
        video capture pipeline with adaptive face-tracking Region of
        Interest (ROI) extraction via MediaPipe Face Mesh~\cite{lugaresi2019mediapipe},
        detrending, zero-phase Butterworth bandpass filtering, and
        peak-based heart rate estimation operating at $\sim$30~FPS.

  \item \textbf{Cross-Modal Clinical Validation.} A statistical
        validation engine that temporally aligns rPPG-derived BPM
        with consumer smartwatch ground truth via cubic interpolation,
        computes MAE, RMSE, and Pearson~$r$, and generates
        Bland--Altman plots conforming to the method-comparison
        standard of~\cite{bland1986statistical}.

  \item \textbf{Biometric-Adaptive LLM Interaction.} A novel
        prompt-injection mechanism whereby real-time RMSSD values
        are embedded into a locally hosted LLM's system prompt,
        enabling the AI assistant to dynamically modulate its
        communicative style---shifting from verbose, technical responses
        during calm states to concise, supportive responses during
        elevated stress~\cite{picard2001affective, lee2024humanai}.
\end{enumerate}

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows: Section~\ref{sec:related}
surveys related work in camera-based physiological sensing and
affective computing. Section~\ref{sec:methodology} details the
proposed signal processing pipeline and adaptive AI architecture.
Section~\ref{sec:results} presents experimental results including
ablation studies under degraded illumination and cognitive stress.
Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses limitations and clinical
applicability, and Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes the paper.

% II. RELATED WORK
% ======================================================================
\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Camera-Based Remote Photoplethysmography}

The seminal demonstration by Verkruysse~\emph{et al.}~\cite{verkruysse2008remote}
established that the cardiac pulse waveform is recoverable from passive
video under ambient light by exploiting differential light absorption in
the hemoglobin-rich dermal microvasculature. Subsequent work by
Poh~\emph{et al.}~\cite{poh2011advancements} introduced Independent
Component Analysis (ICA)-based source separation for webcam rPPG,
achieving mean errors below~3~BPM in controlled laboratory settings.
McDuff~\emph{et al.}~\cite{mcduff2017survey} extended the analysis to
pulse waveform morphology, enabling diastolic and systolic peak
detection from the remote signal. Li~\emph{et al.}~\cite{li2014remote}
proposed adaptive filtering approaches for motion-robust rPPG,
while Chen and McDuff~\cite{chen2018deepphys} demonstrated deep
learning-based rPPG extraction using convolutional attention mechanisms.

Despite these advances, prior systems exhibit several persistent
limitations: (i)~the majority rely on rigid, rectangular ROI selection
rather than adaptive, per-frame face tracking; (ii)~few provide
rigorous Bland--Altman agreement analysis against a reference modality,
instead reporting only Pearson correlation, which is insufficient for
clinical method comparison~\cite{bland1986statistical}; and
(iii)~performance characterization under degraded illumination
remains sparse.

\subsection{Affective Computing and Physiological Feedback}

Affective computing, as formalized by Picard~\cite{picard2001affective},
encompasses computational systems that recognize, interpret, and respond
to human emotional states. The dominant paradigm in commercial affective
technology continues to rely on facial expression classification via
deep convolutional networks~\cite{mollahosseini2017affectnet}. However,
the ecological validity of expression-based inference is increasingly
questioned, as facial displays are known to be culturally modulated and
subject to voluntary control~\cite{barrett2019emotional}.

An emerging alternative leverages \emph{physiological biomarkers}---heart
rate (HR), HRV, electrodermal activity (EDA), and respiratory rate---as
objective indices of affective state. Specifically, the RMSSD metric,
computed from inter-beat interval (IBI) sequences, is a validated marker
of parasympathetic cardiac modulation: a reduction in RMSSD under
cognitive load reflects sympathetic activation and increased
stress~\cite{shaffer2017overview, thayer2012meta}.

\subsection{Adaptive Human--AI Interaction}

The integration of real-time physiological feedback into AI agent
behaviour remains a nascent research direction. Lee~\emph{et al.}
\cite{lee2024humanai} provide a systematic review of systems that
modulate conversational agent responses based on biometric inputs,
noting that most implementations operate on pre-recorded data rather
than live telemetry. Recent work on large language models (LLMs) has
demonstrated the effectiveness of prompt engineering for controlling
output style and content~\cite{brown2020language, wei2022chain},
yet physiological context injection into LLM prompts for real-time
adaptive behaviour remains unexplored. The \textsc{AURA} framework
addresses this gap by performing prompt-level injection of live HRV
state into a locally hosted LLM, enabling sub-second adaptation of
communicative style without fine-tuning or model retraining.

\subsection{Multi-Threaded Video Capture and ROI Extraction}

The system employs a \texttt{CameraSensor} module that acquires frames
in a dedicated daemon thread, decoupled from the application event loop
via thread-safe locks. Each frame is timestamped using monotonic
\texttt{perf\_counter} clocks to ensure nanosecond-resolution temporal
alignment immune to NTP and system clock adjustments.

The captured frames are processed by a \texttt{FaceTracker} module that
performs MediaPipe-based facial mesh detection~\cite{lugaresi2019mediapipe}
with 468 three-dimensional landmarks to dynamically define three
Regions of Interest (ROIs)---the forehead and bilateral cheek
regions---from which the spatial mean of the green channel is extracted
by a \texttt{SignalExtractor} module.

This adaptive ROI strategy addresses a key limitation of prior rPPG
systems that rely on fixed, rectangular forehead regions, which are
fragile under head pose variation and partial occlusion. The use of
three spatially distributed ROIs enables signal averaging across
non-overlapping dermal regions, improving robustness against localized
motion artifacts. Head pose estimation (pitch, yaw, roll) is
simultaneously computed from the facial mesh landmarks, and a motion
flag is raised when angular velocity exceeds a configurable threshold,
signaling potential artifact contamination.

\subsection{Signal Conditioning: Butterworth Bandpass Filtering}

The raw green-channel time series is conditioned through a two-stage
detrending and filtering pipeline:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Detrending.} Linear trend removal via
        \texttt{scipy.signal.detrend}, followed by rolling-mean
        subtraction with a 1.5-second window to eliminate slow
        oscillatory drift from ambient illumination changes and
        respiratory modulation.

  \item \textbf{Bandpass filtering.} A fourth-order Butterworth
        bandpass filter, implemented as a zero-phase
        (\texttt{filtfilt}) design to eliminate phase distortion.
\end{enumerate}

The Butterworth filter transfer function is defined as:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:butterworth}
|H(j\omega)|^{2} = \frac{1}{1 + \left(\frac{\omega}{\omega_c}\right)^{2N}}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $N = 4$ is the filter order and $\omega_c$ represents
the cutoff frequency. The passband is set to $[0.7, 3.0]$~Hz,
corresponding to a physiological heart rate range of 42--180~BPM.
Nyquist-normalized cutoff frequencies are computed dynamically from the
measured sampling rate $f_s$ of the camera:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:nyquist}
W_n = \frac{[f_{\text{low}},\; f_{\text{high}}]}{f_s / 2}
\end{equation}

The zero-phase implementation applies the filter bidirectionally,
resulting in an effective order of $2N = 8$, which provides $\sim$48~dB/octave
roll-off and eliminates temporal distortion critical for accurate
peak localization.

\subsection{Peak Detection and HRV Computation}

Systolic peaks are detected in the filtered signal using
\texttt{scipy.signal.find\_peaks} with two physiologically motivated
constraints: (i)~a minimum inter-peak distance derived from the
maximum expected heart rate ($180$~BPM $\Rightarrow$ minimum
$f_s \times 60/180$ samples), and (ii)~an adaptive prominence
threshold set to 30\% of the filtered signal's standard deviation.

From the detected peaks, Inter-Beat Intervals (IBI) are computed as:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ibi}
\text{IBI}_k = t_{\text{peak}_{k+1}} - t_{\text{peak}_k}
\end{equation}

\noindent and instantaneous BPM is derived as:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bpm}
\text{BPM}_k = \frac{60}{\text{IBI}_k}
\end{equation}

IBI values are validated against physiologically plausible bounds
($0.33$--$1.5$~s, corresponding to 40--180~BPM), and outliers are
rejected prior to aggregate computation. The Root Mean Square of
Successive Differences (RMSSD) is computed as the primary time-domain
HRV metric:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:rmssd}
\text{RMSSD} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{M-1} \sum_{k=1}^{M-1}
  \left(\text{IBI}_{k+1} - \text{IBI}_k\right)^{2}}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $M$ is the total number of valid IBI values.  RMSSD is
interpreted as a marker of vagal (parasympathetic) cardiac modulation:
values below~20~ms indicate elevated sympathetic drive consistent
with acute cognitive stress, while values above~50~ms suggest strong
vagal tone and relaxation~\cite{shaffer2017overview, thayer2012meta}.

\subsection{Biometric-Adaptive LLM Prompt Injection}

The affective feedback loop constitutes the novel contribution of the
\textsc{AURA} architecture.  An \texttt{AffectiveState} module
maintains a rolling 30-second window of BPM and RMSSD values and
classifies the user's cognitive state using a finite state machine (FSM)
with hysteresis-based thresholds to prevent rapid oscillation at boundary
values:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{CALM:} BPM $\leq 85$ and RMSSD $\geq 30$~ms.
        \textit{Exit condition:} BPM~$> 92$ or RMSSD~$< 22$~ms.
  \item \textbf{STRESSED:} BPM $> 85$ or RMSSD $< 30$~ms.
        \textit{Exit condition:} BPM~$\leq 78$ and RMSSD~$\geq 38$~ms.
\end{itemize}

The separate enter/leave thresholds implement hysteresis that prevents
rapid calm$\leftrightarrow$stressed oscillation at boundary values,
a common failure mode in threshold-based classifiers.

The classified state is injected directly into the system prompt of a
locally hosted LLM (Ollama~\cite{ollama2024}, running lightweight
models such as Qwen2 0.5B) via structured prompt augmentation:

\begin{quote}
  \textit{``The user's current physiological state is: [STATE].
  When the user is CALM, provide detailed, technical explanations.
  When the user is STRESSED, respond with concise, supportive language
  and break complex information into smaller steps.''}
\end{quote}

This prompt-injection approach requires no model fine-tuning, no
additional training data, and operates with sub-second latency, as
the state classification is computed from the continuously maintained
physiological buffer. The LLM inference is offloaded to a thread pool
executor to prevent blocking the asynchronous FastAPI event loop.

\subsection{Full-Stack Web Architecture}

The \textsc{AURA} system is deployed as a full-stack web application
comprising a Python FastAPI~\cite{ramirez2018fastapi} backend and a
React~18 frontend built with Vite. The backend exposes four endpoints:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{GET /video\_feed} --- MJPEG multipart stream at
        $\sim$30~FPS with face mesh and ROI overlay.
  \item \texttt{WS /ws/telemetry} --- WebSocket broadcasting JSON
        telemetry (BPM, RMSSD, state, pulse waveform) at 10~Hz.
  \item \texttt{POST /api/chat} --- Asynchronous LLM chat with
        physiological context injection.
  \item \texttt{GET /api/health} --- System health status.
\end{itemize}

The frontend renders real-time biometric dashboards using Recharts
for pulse waveform visualization. All processing occurs locally on the
user's machine with zero external API calls, ensuring complete privacy
of physiological data.

\section{Experimental Setup and Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Experimental Protocol}

An automated experimental suite was developed to systematically evaluate
\textsc{AURA} across three controlled scenarios:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Mode~A --- Baseline.} 60-second recording under
        neutral ambient illumination with the subject seated and
        stationary. No cognitive load applied. ($n = 5$ trials.)

  \item \textbf{Mode~B --- Illumination Stress.} 60-second recording
        under deliberately reduced (dim) lighting conditions to
        challenge the optical sensor's signal-to-noise ratio (SNR).
        ($n = 5$ trials.)

  \item \textbf{Mode~C --- Cognitive Stress.} 60-second recording under
        normal illumination with concurrent mental arithmetic challenges
        (adaptive difficulty) displayed on-screen to induce sympathetic
        activation. ($n = 5$ trials.)
\end{itemize}

For each trial, a consumer smartwatch (wrist-worn PPG) provided
simultaneous ground-truth BPM measurements.  A total of 15 trials
(5 per scenario) were recorded and aggregated into a master dataset.
All experiments were conducted using a standard USB webcam (Logitech
C920) at $640 \times 480$ resolution.

in prior rPPG literature~\cite{poh2011advancements}. The Pearson
correlation coefficient exceeded $r = 0.96$ ($p < 0.001$), indicating
very strong linear agreement between the contactless rPPG signal and
the wrist-worn PPG ground truth.

\subsection{Bland--Altman Validation}

Fig.~\ref{fig:bland_altman} presents the Bland--Altman agreement
analysis between rPPG-derived and smartwatch BPM measurements
under baseline conditions. The mean bias of $+0.85$~BPM with 95\%
Limits of Agreement (LoA) spanning $[-4.12, +5.82]$~BPM confirms
the absence of proportional bias and supports clinical equivalence
of the contactless measurement against the wrist-worn reference,
satisfying the Bland--Altman criteria of~\cite{bland1986statistical}.

\subsection{Ablation Study: Illumination Degradation}

The illumination stress condition (Mode~B) induced a
$3.1~\times$ increase in MAE ($2.15 \rightarrow 6.56$~BPM) and a
$2.5~\times$ reduction in signal SNR ($4.57 \rightarrow 2.03$~dB),
as shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:lighting_ablation}. This degradation is
expected, as the optical rPPG signal amplitude is directly proportional
to incident illumination intensity, and the Butterworth filter cannot
recover information lost below the noise floor.  However, the system
maintained temporal tracking capability with no trial failures,
demonstrating graceful degradation rather than catastrophic failure
under adverse lighting conditions.

\subsection{Cognitive Stress Response}

Under cognitive stress (Mode~C), mean heart rate increased from
$73.6$~BPM (baseline) to $86.4$~BPM, a $17.4\%$ elevation consistent
with sympathetic activation during mental arithmetic tasks.
Concurrently, the RMSSD metric decreased from $45.7$~ms to
$29.1$~ms---a $36.3\%$ reduction---confirming the expected
withdrawal of parasympathetic vagal modulation under cognitive
load (Fig.~\ref{fig:stress_response}).  This RMSSD shift was found to
be statistically significant ($p = 0.003$, paired $t$-test), validating
the framework's capacity to discriminate calm from stressed states using
contactless physiological sensing alone.

\section{Discussion and Limitations}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Clinical Significance}

The achieved baseline MAE of 2.15~BPM positions \textsc{AURA} within
the accuracy envelope of consumer wrist-worn devices, which themselves
exhibit reported MAEs of 2--5~BPM against ECG
reference~\cite{bent2020investigating}. The Bland--Altman analysis
(Fig.~\ref{fig:bland_altman}) reveals a narrow bias with symmetric
Limits of Agreement, indicating that the rPPG measurement can be
substituted for wrist-worn PPG in non-critical wellness monitoring
applications. The system's capacity to discriminate cognitive states
through contactless RMSSD measurement ($p = 0.003$) opens potential
applications in remote workplace stress monitoring, telehealth
screening, and adaptive educational environments.

\subsection{Illumination Sensitivity}

The $3.1\times$ MAE increase under dim lighting constitutes the primary
operational limitation of the framework.  The Butterworth filter's
cardiac passband isolation is effective only when the input signal's
SNR exceeds a minimum threshold; below this, the filter amplifies
noise within the passband. Potential mitigations include: (i)~adaptive
gain control on the green channel prior to filtering, (ii)~integration
of near-infrared (NIR) illumination for controlled-light environments,
and (iii)~multi-wavelength chrominance-based rPPG methods such as
CHROM~\cite{dehaan2013robust} that exploit the decorrelation between
cardiac and illumination-induced variations.

\subsection{Affective State Classification}

The binary (CALM/STRESSED) classification with hysteresis thresholds
employed in the LLM prompt-injection module represents a deliberately
simplified affective model.  While sufficient for demonstrating the
biometric-adaptive concept, clinical deployment would require a
multi-dimensional affective taxonomy incorporating arousal--valence
axes, anxiety-specific biomarkers (e.g., respiratory sinus arrhythmia),
and longitudinal baseline calibration for inter-individual variability
in resting HRV.

\subsection{Limitations}

Several limitations merit explicit acknowledgment:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Single-subject validation.} The current experimental
        protocol evaluates a single subject across 15 trials. A
        multi-participant study with demographic diversity is required
        to establish population-level generalizability.

  \item \textbf{Skin tone sensitivity.} rPPG signal amplitude is
        inversely related to melanin concentration in the dermal layers.
        The current system has not been validated across Fitzpatrick
        skin types IV--VI, and performance degradation in darker skin
        tones is a known limitation of green-channel rPPG
        methods~\cite{nowara2020meta}.

  \item \textbf{Motion artifact rejection.} While the framework
        detects motion flags via head pose estimation, it does not
        implement active artifact rejection (e.g., adaptive filtering
        or robust PCA). Excessive head movement during signal
        acquisition remains a source of error.

  \item \textbf{LLM response quality.} The effectiveness of the
        biometric-adaptive prompt injection depends on the underlying
        LLM's instruction-following capability. No formal evaluation
        of user-perceived quality or task completion efficiency was
        conducted in this study.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper presented \textsc{AURA}, a contactless rPPG framework that
unifies real-time cardiovascular monitoring, rigorous cross-modal
validation, and biometric-adaptive Human--AI interaction within a
privacy-preserving, locally deployed full-stack web application. The
system extracts heart rate and RMSSD-based HRV from a standard webcam
video stream, achieves clinical-grade accuracy ($\text{MAE} = 2.15$~BPM,
$r > 0.96$) under controlled conditions, and demonstrates robust
cognitive stress detection via a statistically significant RMSSD
reduction under cognitive load ($p = 0.003$).

The introduction of a physiological prompt-injection loop---whereby live
RMSSD values modulate an LLM's communicative style in real time---represents
a novel contribution to the intersection of biomedical signal processing
and adaptive AI systems. This mechanism requires no model fine-tuning,
operates at sub-second latency, and is architecture-agnostic with respect
to the underlying LLM.

Future work will pursue the following directions:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Multi-participant validation} across diverse demographic
        groups and Fitzpatrick skin types to establish population-level
        generalizability.
  \item \textbf{Multi-wavelength rPPG} integration using chrominance-based
        methods (CHROM, POS) for improved illumination robustness.
  \item \textbf{Multi-dimensional affective model} incorporating
        arousal--valence axes with longitudinal baseline tracking for
        personalized stress profiling.
  \item \textbf{User study evaluation} measuring the perceived quality
        and task-completion efficiency of biometric-adaptive LLM
        responses compared to static interaction.
\end{enumerate}
